## The Gap Nobody Warns You About

When I co-founded Neuroelectrics in 2011, I was confident that the science was compelling. We had strong evidence that non-invasive electrical brain stimulation could modulate cortical excitability. The physics was tractable. The computational models were improving. Clinical need was enormous.

What I underestimated was everything else.

## Regulatory Reality

The path from a promising research tool to an approved medical device is not a straight line — it is a labyrinth. FDA IDE applications, CE marking, GCP compliance, IRB protocols. Each step requires documentation, iteration, and an organizational discipline that is entirely different from running a research group.

We made every mistake. We under-resourced our regulatory team early on. We ran studies with endpoints that were scientifically interesting but not optimally chosen for regulatory approval. We had to redesign trials partway through.

None of this invalidated the science. But it added years.

## What Kept Us Going

Two things.

First, the patients. Every time I visited a clinical site and met someone with drug-resistant epilepsy who had run out of options, the urgency became concrete. Abstraction dissolves in the presence of human suffering.

Second, the community. The researchers who partnered with us — neurologists, psychiatrists, biomedical engineers — were not just customers. They were collaborators who shared the conviction that this technology had genuine potential.

## A Different Relationship with Time

I have learned to think in longer arcs. The EEG was invented in 1929. Transcranial stimulation research took off in the 1990s. We are still in the early innings of understanding what is possible.

The right response to this is not impatience. It is better questions, better tools, and the humility to let the biology teach you what you did not know you needed to learn.
