## 1929: A Physicist Listens to the Brain

Hans Berger was looking for the physical correlate of psychic energy when he made the first electroencephalographic recording of a human brain in 1929. His motivations were frankly mystical. His methods were rigorous. The result — the discovery that the brain produces measurable electrical rhythms — was one of the most consequential observations in the history of science.

Berger identified the alpha rhythm: a sinusoidal oscillation near 10 Hz that dominates the occipital scalp when subjects close their eyes and relax. Nearly a century later, the alpha rhythm still challenges our full understanding. Why does it exist? What function does it serve? Why does it disappear during visual processing?

## The Physics of Volume Conduction

To read the brain electrically, you must first understand the physics. Neural currents generated by synchronous populations of neurons travel through cerebrospinal fluid, skull, and scalp before reaching our electrodes. This "volume conduction" smears and distorts the signal.

Developing accurate forward models — mathematical descriptions of how neural sources project to the scalp — is essential for making EEG recordings interpretable. Our group has worked for over two decades on improving these models, incorporating realistic head geometry derived from MRI and better accounts of tissue conductivity.

## Why Resolution Is Not Everything

There is a common criticism of EEG: its spatial resolution cannot compete with fMRI. This is true. But it misses the point.

EEG measures electrical activity directly, in real time, at millisecond resolution. The brain computes on timescales of milliseconds, not seconds. For understanding the dynamics of neural circuits — how rhythms arise, interact, and reorganize — temporal resolution is not a weakness but a fundamental strength.

Moreover, EEG is portable, affordable, and non-invasive. It can be worn outside the laboratory. It can monitor brain states continuously. For both research and clinical applications, these properties are decisive.

## The Road Ahead

Modern high-density EEG systems with 64 to 256 electrodes, combined with machine learning analysis pipelines, are transforming what is possible. We can now decode imagined movements, detect epileptic focus, track anaesthetic depth, and monitor the effects of brain stimulation in real time.

Berger would not recognize the technology. I believe he would recognize the ambition.
